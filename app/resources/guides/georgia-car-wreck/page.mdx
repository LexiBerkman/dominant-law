## What to do at the scene (if safe)

Prioritize safety and emergency response first. If you are physically able, capture key facts while details are fresh and conditions are unchanged.

### High-value records to capture

- Vehicle positions and damage from multiple angles.
- License plates and visible company identifiers.
- Road conditions, lighting, and traffic controls.
- Witness names and contact information.

## Medical treatment and documentation strategy

Early and consistent medical records often become central timeline evidence. Keep notes factual and consistent with your care team.

### Keep one organized file

- Visit summaries and test results.
- Prescriptions and treatment plans.
- Out-of-pocket receipts and mileage logs.
- Work-impact notes (missed days, restrictions).

## Insurance communication playbook

Insurance adjusters may request statements quickly. General best practice is concise, factual communication with documented follow-up.

### Do

- Confirm basic facts.
- Request claim identifiers in writing.
- Keep a call log (date, time, topic, next step).

### Do not

- Guess about fault percentages.
- Minimize or exaggerate injuries.
- Provide unnecessary speculation.

## Georgia-specific notes

Timelines can be short and law can change. Verify current Georgia statutes, notice requirements, and court rules before relying on generalized summaries.

## When to call a lawyer

Call sooner if injuries are significant, liability is disputed, multiple insurers are involved, or communication pressure rises.
