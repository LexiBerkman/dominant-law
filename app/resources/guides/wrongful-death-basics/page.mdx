## First 72 hours: a practical approach

Families often face urgent calls, paperwork, and emotional overload. A structured process can reduce risk and preserve options.

### Stabilize communication

Choose one family contact for external communications when possible. This limits conflicting statements and repeated stress.

### Preserve core records

Keep a secure list of reports, providers, claim numbers, and all written correspondence.

## Evidence and documentation priorities

Wrongful death matters are fact-intensive. Organized records can materially improve timeline clarity and legal evaluation.

### Suggested record categories

- Incident and investigative records.
- Medical and death-related documentation.
- Financial records relevant to support and loss analysis.
- Communication logs with insurers and involved parties.

## Insurance and third-party communications

Insurers may request interviews or authorizations quickly. General practice is factual, concise communication and documented follow-up.

### Do

- Ask for requests in writing.
- Keep date/time logs for every contact.
- Preserve all letters, emails, and claim references.

### Do not

- Speculate about legal conclusions.
- Sign broad releases without review.
- Share confidential details through unsecured channels.

## Georgia-specific note

Timelines and legal requirements can be short and situation-specific. Verify current Georgia law and procedure before relying on generalized summaries.

## When to call a lawyer now

Escalate quickly if liability is disputed, multiple parties are involved, a lawsuit is threatened, or insurer pressure increases.
