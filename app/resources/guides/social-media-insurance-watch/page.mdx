## Why social media creates claim risk

Insurers and defense counsel look for inconsistencies between posts and claim allegations. Even harmless context can be reframed to challenge credibility.

## High-risk content categories

### Activity and lifestyle posts

Photos or videos of physical activity can be used to suggest your injuries are less serious than documented.

### Casual statements about the incident

Offhand comments can be interpreted as admissions or contradictions.

### Tagged content and comments

Risk is not limited to what you post directly. Tagged media and comment threads can also become relevant.

## What plaintiffs should do instead

### Communication guardrails

- Keep case details offline.
- Use direct private channels with your legal team.
- If asked about the case, decline public discussion and use our [insurance adjuster guide](/resources/guides/insurance-adjusters) to keep claim communications disciplined.

### Documentation best practices

- Keep treatment and symptom updates in your private case file, not social media.
- Save claim communications and legal correspondence in one organized location.

## Critical warning: do not destroy evidence

Do not mass-delete posts or alter potential evidence without legal guidance. Preservation obligations may apply, and improper deletion can cause separate legal issues.

## Bottom line

Treat every post as potentially reviewable by insurance and defense counsel. A disciplined social media strategy can protect your claim integrity, especially when paired with clean [medical documentation](/resources/guides/medical-documentation) and consistent off-platform communication.
