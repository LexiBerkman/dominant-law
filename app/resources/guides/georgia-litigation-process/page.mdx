## Phase 1: intake, strategy, and preservation

The process usually begins with intake, a high-level fact review, and immediate risk spotting. At this stage, the priority is not theatrics. It is control. That means preserving evidence, identifying insurance, building a reliable timeline, and making sure the client is not making preventable mistakes in treatment or communication.

If the injury came from a crash, our [Georgia car wreck guide](/resources/guides/georgia-car-wreck) explains the first-day evidence steps in more detail. If the claim involves insurer communication pressure, our [insurance adjuster guide](/resources/guides/insurance-adjusters) is the companion page.

## Phase 2: treatment and factual development

Most injury cases need a stable treatment record before anyone can evaluate damages with confidence. During this phase, records are collected, symptoms are tracked, and the legal team works to understand liability, damages, and defenses.

### What usually happens here

- Medical records and billing are gathered.
- Witnesses, reports, and photos are organized.
- Insurance coverage and policy issues are reviewed.
- The case theory starts to sharpen.

This is where disciplined [medical documentation](/resources/guides/medical-documentation) matters. Weak record organization creates avoidable friction later in litigation.

## Phase 3: pre-suit demand and negotiation

Not every case should file suit immediately. In some matters, the right move is to finish core investigation, package the claim well, and present a pre-suit demand. The goal is not to send paper for its own sake. The goal is to show facts, damages, and litigation readiness clearly enough that settlement pressure becomes real.

### Why some cases still move into litigation

- Liability is disputed.
- Damages are undervalued.
- The insurer delays or lowballs the claim.
- Deadlines are approaching.
- Multiple parties or complicated facts require formal discovery.

## Phase 4: filing the lawsuit

When pre-suit efforts are not enough, the next step is filing a complaint in the appropriate Georgia court before the statute of limitations or other deadlines expire. After filing, the defendant must be served and will usually file an answer.

Filing suit does not mean trial is guaranteed. It means the case has moved into a formal process with court rules, deadlines, and discovery obligations.

## Phase 5: written discovery

Once the case is in litigation, the parties usually exchange written discovery. This can include interrogatories, requests for production, and requests for admission. The point is to force information exchange, pin down positions, and identify where disputes actually are.

### Common discovery topics

- Medical treatment history.
- Prior injuries and claimed limitations.
- Liability facts and scene details.
- Insurance positions and defenses.
- Economic loss documentation.

## Phase 6: depositions

Depositions are sworn question-and-answer sessions taken outside the courtroom. Clients, defendants, witnesses, treating providers, corporate representatives, and experts may all be deposed depending on the case.

For many clients, this is the first part of litigation that feels especially real. Preparation matters. A clean record, clear timeline, and disciplined communication style make a major difference here.

## Phase 7: experts, motions, and case pressure

As the case matures, expert issues and motions can begin shaping leverage. Some cases need accident reconstruction, medical causation analysis, economic damages work, or industry-specific experts. At the same time, one or both sides may file motions about evidence, legal claims, or case scope.

This phase often determines whether a case is likely to settle or continue toward trial.

## Phase 8: mediation and settlement efforts

Many Georgia injury cases go through mediation before trial. Mediation is a structured settlement process, usually with a neutral mediator, designed to test positions and try to resolve the matter.

### What clients should expect at mediation

- Negotiation usually moves in rounds, not one conversation.
- Opening offers may be far from realistic value.
- A case can settle the same day, shortly after, or not at all.
- Trial readiness still matters, even in mediation.

## Phase 9: trial preparation

If the case does not resolve, trial preparation intensifies. Witness outlines are refined, exhibits are organized, motions are argued, and the story of the case must become clear enough for a jury to follow.

This is where earlier discipline pays off. Cases that were organized from intake are easier to present cleanly.

## Phase 10: trial, verdict, and what happens after

At trial, both sides present evidence, question witnesses, and argue the case to a judge or jury. If there is a verdict or a settlement after suit, the process still does not end immediately.

### Post-resolution steps can include

- Final settlement paperwork.
- Lien or subrogation review.
- Case expenses and fee accounting.
- Disbursement timing.
- Potential post-trial motions or appeals in some matters.

## Bottom line

The Georgia litigation process is not one meeting or one court date. It is a sequence of pressure points. Strong cases are usually built through preparation, documentation, and disciplined pacing from the beginning.
